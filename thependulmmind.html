The Pendulum of the Mind

Why can the mind not stay in the same state for a long time? When the mind remains calm for some time, it starts craving fast and stimulating activities, such as listening to loud music, scrolling reels, or exploring something exciting. Yet, after getting tired of this fast-paced life, the mind again starts craving calmness and silence.

Why does the mind keep shifting between calmness and stimulation? Here is the explanation of this behavior according to psychology, philosophy, and today’s lifestyle.

1. Psychology: The Nervous System Hates Static States

Your mind is not designed to stay in one state forever. It oscillates: Calm → Stimulation → Fatigue → Calm. This is not a flaw; it is regulation.

Homeostasis and Arousal

The brain tries to keep arousal in a “just right” zone, known as the Optimal Arousal Theory. It acts like a thermostat that cannot tolerate extremes:

  • Too calm for too long: You become under-stimulated. The brain perceives this as boredom and begins craving novelty.
  • Too stimulated for too long: You become overloaded. The brain perceives this as stress and begins craving silence.

When calm lasts, dopamine drops and novelty-seeking circuits wake up. You feel, "I need something intense." When chaos lasts, cortisol stays high, the nervous system fatigues, and the body demands rest.

"Your mind is not indecisive. It is regulating."

Dopamine vs. Happiness

Fast things give dopamine spikes, not peace. Calm gives serotonin and GABA, not excitement. Dopamine is about seeking, not enjoying. Once dopamine is high for too long, the brain downregulates. Then, calm starts to feel empty instead of peaceful.

Modern brains confuse this biological cycle with "I’m changing as a person." You’re not. Chemistry is just doing its job.

2. Philosophy: The Mind is Movement

Buddhism

The Buddha was brutally honest: The mind is restless by nature. Suffering comes from expecting it not to be.

  • The desire for stimulation after calm is tanha (craving).
  • The desire for calm after stimulation is the same craving wearing a different costume.

The problem is not movement. The problem is attachment to states. You want calm to stay forever, and you want excitement to stay forever. In reality, both are temporary. Clinging creates frustration.

Taoism

Taoism would suggest that Yin (Stillness) gives birth to Yang (Movement), and movement gives birth back to stillness. Wanting one permanently breaks the flow. The mistake is asking, "Why can’t I stay calm?" The wiser question is, "Why do I resist the shift?"

Existential Angle

The mind seeks intensity because intensity gives meaning. Silence removes distraction, and suddenly you meet yourself. Many people escape calm not because it’s boring, but because it’s revealing.

3. Today’s Lifestyle: The Violent Swing

Here’s where things get ugly. Your nervous system evolved for walking, talking, and silence. But today we feed it loud music, infinite scrolling, and constant novelty with zero recovery time.

So the natural wave becomes extreme. Silence is seen as unbearable boredom. Stimulation becomes addiction-level intensity. This is not curiosity anymore. This is dopamine debt.

4. An Important Correction

You are assuming that the calm state is the "right" state. It’s not. Calm is just low activation. Stimulation is just high activation.

"Freedom is not staying calm. Freedom is not being dragged by the swing."

A stable mind still changes states. An unstable mind panics about the change.

5. What Maturity Actually Looks Like

Maturity is not permanent calm. It is noticing the craving without obeying it immediately. It is allowing energy without feeding it junk, and allowing calm without forcing it to be productive.

A trained mind says: "I feel the pull. I don’t need to act on it right now." That is regulation. That is adulthood. That is what monks and athletes secretly share.

Final Truth

Your mind isn’t broken. It is adaptive, restless, and badly trained by modern life. The real problem is not the switching; it’s the impatience with switching.

When calm comes, you try to use it. When excitement comes, you try to escape it. Both are forms of control. Both fail. The mind settles only when you stop demanding that it stay anywhere.
